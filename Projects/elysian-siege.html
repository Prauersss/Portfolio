About the Project

What the game is, which parts I built, and what went wrong along the way

The Game

Elysian Siege was our final project for the HND, built by a small team over nine months. You play a protagonist fighting her way towards her objective, picking up abilities and items that change how you approach each encounter. Since it was a team project we each owned different systems — the sections below cover the parts that were mine.

What I Worked On

Enemy AI & Combat

I built the ranged enemies: detecting the player, deciding when to shoot, and reacting to the environment. A lot of the work was tuning how aggressive they are so fights feel tense without being unfair.

Audio Integration

I handled all the audio, in menus and in gameplay: background music, sound effects, and the little feedback sounds that tell you something happened.

Visual Effects

I made the particle effects for explosions, enemy deaths and pickups — the kind of detail that makes hitting things feel good.

Collectibles System

Every pickup in the game is mine, each with its own benefit, plus the curve-based animation they follow when you collect them.

Pool System

I wrote an object pool that pre-instantiates and reuses pickups, enemies and particles, activating them based on distance to the player. It's what lets the big levels run without the framerate falling apart.

Problems I Ran Into

AI Behavior Balance

Gameplay Programming
The problem

My first versions of the ranged enemies were either too aggressive — frustrating — or too passive — boring. Neither felt fair, and it threw off the pacing of every fight.

How I fixed it

I split the behavior into states — patrol, detection, combat — and added some randomness to the shooting patterns. That keeps encounters readable but never quite predictable.

Audio Synchronization

Technical Integration
The problem

Sounds firing late or out of sync with what was happening on screen. A delayed hit sound is a small thing, but it made the whole combat feel disconnected.

How I fixed it

I centralized everything in an audio manager with pooled audio sources, so each effect plays the moment its gameplay event fires instead of waiting on instantiation.

Performance Optimization

Technical Optimization
The problem

The particle effects were tanking the framerate, especially in big fights with several explosions and pickups on screen at once.

How I fixed it

I moved the particle systems into the object pool and scaled the particle count down with distance from the camera. Far-away explosions don't need all that confetti.

Pickup Animation Flow

User Experience
The problem

Pickup animations had to feel rewarding without getting in the way — and my early attempts kept causing collision problems with the collection logic mid-animation.

How I fixed it

I ended up animating them with CurveVector for a natural arc, turning their collision off while the animation runs, and scaling the visual feedback with the pickup's value.
